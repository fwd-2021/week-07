Research

Research Prospectus

This document outlines the core topic areas and a selection of planned and in-progress projects that constitute the next three years of my research agenda. As of Fall 2020, I will conduct this research as a full-time tenured associate professor in pursuit of eventual promotion to full professor in the Department of Information Technology and Management, in the new College of Computing at Illinois Institute of Technology.

The primary contributions of my research are:

  1. to create innovative field- and industry-leading practices and methodologies for web development based on the leading edge of published open web-standards, and
  2. to better educate and professionalize current and next-generation web developers.

Taken together, my primary research contributions improve the professional status and expertise of web developers, leading to improvements in the overall stability, sophistication, and accessibility of the web. My research closely aligns with research in information technology not only in its technical subject matter but also in its relevance to both industry and higher education. As with all of my existing published work, my research will continue to address directly those who teach and lead others along a broad continuum of mentoring and instructional situations, including formal university education and lifelong, continuing education expected of professional developers.

Core Assumptions and Approaches

Web development as a professional practice is pulled in opposite directions by two unsustainable, contradictory extremes: one extreme is marked by inertia and stagnation, and the other by instability and fragmentation.

In the first extreme, web developers and certain behemoth frameworks (e.g., Bootstrap, jQuery, and Drupal—to name prominent front- and back-end examples) perpetuate, often unwittingly, web development practices inextricably entangled with outdated hacks, antipatterns, and browser-specific workarounds. Virtually all of the practices and concepts found at this extreme were, at an earlier time in the web’s history, unavoidable side-effects of doing development in a browser landscape rife with incomplete and incompatible implementations of JavaScript, the Document Object Model (DOM), and even HTML and CSS. In spite of the web gradually maturing in recent years to include widespread, cross-browser support for web standards (that is, the recommendations issued by the World Wide Web Consortium, W3C, as well as true standards published by Ecma International, ISO, and other groups), outdated web-development practices and techniques remain widespread today in routine development approaches and, worse, continue to be perpetuated even in university and professional education.

At the other extreme, web development warps and fragments into instability signaled by crowds of developers making leaps from one framework or development methodology du jour to the next, at a breakneck pace. Knowledge of a framework becomes the defining developer ambition and end in itself. Next to the burnout that developers report experiencing in trying to maintain such a pace, the most professionally dire side-effect of this extreme is developers claiming to “know” a framework but not the language it’s written in or the protocols and APIs the framework touches: the developer who claims, “I know React, but not JavaScript,” for example. Developer efforts to stay current with the latest, most fashionable frameworks means the neglect or abandonment of bedrock principles and sensible, user-centered constraints—such as usability and accessibility—that are fundamental to web development. Among the most important principles that are missing from developer practice at this extreme are progressive enhancement, graceful degradation, and the separation of concerns inherent in the web’s three core front-end languages, HTML, CSS, and JavaScript.

The actual professional education and practices of individual web developers, of course, are to be found between those two extremes. But unsurprisingly, developers themselves are routinely pulled in opposite directions by parallel extremes of inherited legacy codebases or functional requirements to support ancient browsers like Internet Explorer 7, on the one side, and managerial demands or requirements listed in job ads, on the other, with companies sometimes demanding that applicants to web-developer positions have numerous years of experience with particular web frameworks or development methodologies that are themselves sometimes only months old. (Yes, that really does happen.)

At the broadest scope, my research is an applied investigation into the extremes of stagnation and fragmentation in web development. I examine and analyze the shortcomings of lingering bad practices and hastily developed new practices, always with regard to new and emergent web standards that also inform my approaches to course design and classroom instruction. In short, I produce innovative research that improves the practices of working developers and enhances the core curricular objectives and outcomes in university settings.

Research Projects

The projects below all build on my twenty-year research record in the areas of web standards and web design and development. Each of the research projects below directly addresses or considers questions and problems of pedagogy with regard to the professionalization and career tracks of students pursuing web development or adjacent areas of computing and information technology. A few of the projects here also consider the development of industry-connected university faculty—especially those who are themselves professional developers first, and faculty second.

  • Book: Pragmatic Teaching & Mentoring. Currently in its proposal stage for submission to Pragmatic Programmers, this peer-reviewed book will fill a tremendous need for providing guidance to developers who find themselves engaged in a broad range of activity that resembles teaching and instruction, including mentoring, delivering conference presentations and workshops, and teaching formal college-level classes. I’m writing the book with ITM’s own industry faculty in mind, as well as former students who write me from time to time with questions about how to mentor and teach. This is a book for people whose primary professional work and identity are tied to industry, but who also lead others through instruction. To provide sound, actionable guidance for this group, the book is anchored in cultural-historical activity theory and threshold concepts. But rather than wading deeply into explicit theory, it offers pragmatic advice derived from theory and my own professional practice. I specifically discuss how to structure, conduct, and self-assess mentoring sessions, conference presentations, and classroom assignments and course designs. For managerial and educational settings that include assessment and review, I provide guidance in establishing instructional and educational approaches that value collaborative learning and formative assessment in addition to, or as a complete substitution for, traditional forms of summative assessment.
  • Short book: HTML Antipatterns. This short peer-reviewed book, intended for publication with leading web-development press A Book Apart, will catalog and demonstrate vastly improved alternatives to hundreds of real-world examples of poor HTML usage. Much of the attention in front-end web development is paid to exciting advances in the CSS specification (CSS Flexible Boxes, also known as Flexbox, and CSS Grid, for two recent examples) and in the EcmaScript and Document Object Model specifications. With far less fanfare but no less importance, HTML and its specification continue to advance, and browser-makers are steadily improving and expanding their implementations of the HTML specification. Developers’ actual use of HTML, however, tends to be stunted by the persistence of old habits and unstudied practices that result in antipatterns: widespread but ineffective, even deleterious solutions and responses to common, recurring problems. I have been collecting and documenting examples of antipatterns found in the wild on the open web as well as in student work from years of teaching web development to students with a very wide range of preparedness. I have also been in contact with a significant figure in the web-standards movement of the late 1990s and early 2000s, to explore collaboratively authoring this project.
  • Conference presentations and collaboratively written articles: Fitting web curricula to the web-development stack. I have been collecting and studying peer institution examples of how to better fit web courses and curricula to the full stack of web development. This project is derived from my ongoing work with faculty on a subcommittee of ITM’s Curriculum Committee charged with modernizing the graduate and undergraduate web curriculum, including the structure and requirements for all web specializations, minors, and certificates. This research project’s central problem is determining how solid or porous the borders should be between front- and back-end courses, and how and to what degree back-end concepts should be introduced in front-end courses, and vice versa. In the near term, I plan to propose an early version of this work for presentation at SIGITE, but it will take its final form as a small series of articles—ideally written collaboratively with other ITM faculty on the web-curriculum subcommittee.
  • Specification development: ARIA in HTML. This project is a direct contribution to the Accessible Rich Internet Applications (ARIA) in HTML specification (https://www.w3.org/TR/html-aria/), whose most current working draft was just published on February 15, 2020. I will be actively participating in the specification’s open development: monitoring and responding to posts on the specification’s GitHub Issues tracker; crafting and submitting pull requests to improve the language of the spec; and testing and reviewing the results of the specification’s conformance-testing suite against the specification itself. This project extends in new ways my long-time research interests in accessibility, as well as my professional ambition to directly contribute to the development of open specifications, which my research is heavily reliant upon.
  • Group-authored article with graduate students: WebRTC. In Fall 2020, I will be teaching a course on the Web Real-Time Communication Between Browsers 1.0 specification (https://www.w3.org/TR/webrtc/, WebRTC), which finally achieved Candidate Recommendation status in December of 2019 (the specification has been in development since May of 2011). I will use that class in part to work towards a publication jointly authored with graduate students, who will work with me on compiling an extensive bibliography of secondary materials on WebRTC for publication as a bibliographic essay or extended literature review in a topically appropriate IEEE or ACM journal. We will include in the project detailed notes and caveats that highlight points where secondary sources deviate significantly from the Candidate Recommendation version of the specification, which has changed in significant, backward-incompatible ways over its nearly decade-long development.
  • Open-source contributions: Mozilla Developer Network (MDN). Finally, I plan to more frequently review and contribute content for the industry-leading web documentation at MDN. I have previously contributed to the documentation’s running text as well as its code examples and compatibility data, and I intend to become more actively present on MDN both to serve the broader web-development community and to model for ITM students the importance of contributing to the maintenance and expansion of accurate, well crafted documentation sets. I will also actively encourage and mentor graduate and advanced undergraduate students looking to make their own MDN contributions.
